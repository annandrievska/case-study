01 — Context

A platform built for people who didn't grow up with a mouse in their hand

NBS is an Israeli B2B e-commerce platform serving municipalities, grocery stores, and community organizations — with one very specific audience constraint: many of their end customers are ultra-Orthodox Jews who don't use smartphones, avoid the internet on religious grounds, and in some cases can't leave the house to shop.

To serve them, NBS built a hybrid commerce system: a web storefront (Shoppi, B2C) for those comfortable online, and an IVR phone-ordering system for everyone else. When you call, a voice menu reads product categories, prices, and promotions — you press numbers to navigate and place orders.

The B2B admin panel is where stores, municipalities, and events businesses configure everything: products, categories, promotions, IVR phone trees, and email campaigns. That admin panel is what I worked on.

🏪

B2B clients

Grocery stores, municipalities, community centers — often managed by non-technical staff who needed to configure complex logic themselves.

📞

IVR as primary channel

For a significant portion of the audience, the phone tree isn't a fallback. It's the only channel. This made IVR configuration mission-critical.

🧩

Stitched-together system

When I joined, the product was a live mix of a decade-old legacy UI and a newer redesign — sometimes both visible on the same page.

🔒

No direct user access

Due to language barriers and cultural norms, I couldn't speak to end users directly. All user insight came through the PM, who acted as a critical bridge.

Scope of work

This case study focuses on three modules where the design challenges were most distinct. But in practice, my work was continuous and broad — redesigning existing sections (product management, customer profiles, sales flows), adding new features to live modules, refining interactions based on ongoing client feedback, and maintaining consistency across a system that was still partly legacy. New and existing were always in parallel.

02 — The Problem

Admins were building phone experiences blind

The IVR module was the most technically complex — and the most broken from a UX standpoint. B2B admins had to configure phone trees of 150+ steps, but the interface gave them almost no visibility into the logic they were creating.

A recurring pattern emerged across almost every module: the initial request was always "we need more options." But when I dug into the actual pain points through the PM, the real issue was almost never missing data — it was missing clarity about the data already there. That shift in framing shaped how I approached the redesign.

1

Navigation blindness in large trees

In a 150+ step IVR, if step 1 leads to step 180, an admin had to manually scroll through a flat table to understand what step 180 does. There was no way to "follow" a path.

2

No "incoming" context

Admins could see where a step goes — but not which other steps point to it. Debugging logic or making changes without this was like editing a map with no compass.

3

Silent audio cascade failures

One audio recording could be used across 10 different IVR steps. Replacing it would silently update all 10 — including ones where the new recording made no sense. Admins had no warning this would happen.

4

Code-speak in the interface

Transition rules showed developer codes like go_tz, n_r, not_active_owner. For a non-technical store manager, these were meaningless — and dangerous to misconfigure.

5

Edit-mode disorientation

When clicking "Edit", the layout would restructure entirely — fields would disappear, reorder, or change type compared to the view mode. Admins repeatedly lost their place mid-task.

6

Missing foundations

Beyond IVR, two other modules had foundational gaps. The admin panel had no category management system at all — no unified place to create or control the hierarchy that structures both Shoppi product browsing and the IVR phone tree. And there was no email capability whatsoever: B2B clients communicated with their customers entirely outside the platform, with no way to measure whether any of it worked.

7

The chicken-and-egg creation problem

To configure a transition in a recording, admins needed to point it at an existing IVR step. But if that step didn't exist yet, they had to abandon the current flow, go create the step separately, then return and finish the transition. There was no way to create a missing step in context — every gap in the tree broke the creation flow.

03 — My Approach

Designing without direct access to users — and making it work

I couldn't run usability tests with the actual users. The community's norms made direct access impossible, and there was a real language barrier on top of that. So I adapted.

PM as research proxy

I worked closely with the PM to structure the feedback she was collecting from clients. I pushed to understand not just "what they complained about" but the specific moment in the workflow where confusion happened. This became my primary qualitative data.

Building the feedback loop from scratch

There was no structured way to capture user pain points when I joined. I pushed for support to start documenting client complaints — categorised by module and workflow stage. This hadn't been done before. It became the primary quantitative signal for prioritisation across the redesign.

PRD as ethnographic document

The PRD contained deep business logic — product types, constraint rules, IVR flow logic, billing behaviour. I read it as a proxy for observing user workflows, tracing every edge case to understand what could go wrong in real admin use.

Reframing the brief

The initial ask was often "add more fields." I tried to bring the conversation back to first principles: is the problem missing information — or missing clarity about information already there? Making that case to the PM and stakeholders shifted several design directions significantly.

Multiple variants, structured decision-making

For each major flow, I produced 2–3 meaningful alternatives — not just visual variations, but different structural approaches. This gave the PM and stakeholders a basis for real decisions rather than approval theatre.

Advocating for user control within technical constraints

In Categories, the PM wanted to restrict certain edit actions to simplify backend work. I made the case that removing control from users doesn't reduce errors — it just makes them unrecoverable. We worked through the implications together and landed on a design that gave users the flexibility they needed while keeping the technical scope manageable.

04a — Solution · IVR Module

Making a phone tree readable by humans

I redesigned the IVR module across four interconnected sections: the Records library (audio files), the Functions library (backend logic blocks), the Rules/Tree view (the step-by-step phone flow), and a new Product Assignments view showing which audio recordings are used across which products.

Navigating a 150-step tree
Before

Flat table, numbered rows only. To understand where step 180 leads, you manually scroll through 179 rows. No visible relationship between steps.

After

Each step shows its name, description, and destination steps inline. Inline labels replace numeric IDs. Admins can trace logic by reading, not scrolling.

The silent audio cascade problem
Before

Replacing a recording updated it everywhere it was used, with no warning. Admins discovered the mistake only after customers called in confused.

After

New "Update Record Assignment" modal: shows current usage, lets admins choose to apply the change globally or only to specific selected steps. A complete replacement for blind bulk edits.

Translating developer codes to admin language
Before

Transition conditions shown as raw codes: go_tz → 3, n_r → 180, not_active_owner → 997. Meaningful to developers. Dangerous to admins.

After

Functions displayed with human-readable descriptions ("Verify caller ID against registered number"). Transition targets show step names, not just IDs. Progressive disclosure hides advanced logic.

Edit-mode disorientation
Before

Clicking Edit reshuffled the layout entirely. Fields changed order, some disappeared. Admins lost their place every time and sometimes edited the wrong field.

After

View and edit modes maintain identical field order and structure. Changes are highlighted inline. State is preserved — admins always know where they are.

"The real design challenge wasn't making it prettier — it was making the consequences of every action visible before the admin committed to it."
04b — Solution · Categories Module

Three-level hierarchies without three levels of confusion

Categories in NBS serve three things at once: product browsing on Shoppi (B2C), the internal ordering of products within the catalogue, and the phone menu structure for IVR callers. Neither surface had a proper category system. I was building it from scratch — for all three, at once.

The core constraint: display order isn't just a sort preference — it's the key a caller presses in the phone menu. The audio recordings themselves are managed separately for now, but the structure set here already shapes the IVR experience. That made every structural decision higher-stakes than a typical admin table — and connecting these modules properly is planned for the next iteration.

The hierarchy is flexible. Products can be added at any level where no sub-sections exist — department, category, or sub-category. A business with a small inventory can place products directly in a department without creating unnecessary levels.

One rule: levels can't be mixed. If a department already has categories inside, products can't be added directly to it — and vice versa.

Pattern: Two interaction modes in one table

The main list uses an expandable accordion — not a tree panel, not nested pages. Clicking the ▶ arrow expands child rows inline for a quick overview. Clicking the name navigates to a dedicated detail page for management actions. Two distinct interactions, two distinct intents — surfaced in the same table.

Pattern: Display order as read-only IVR key

The order number is shown as a read-only badge, not an editable input. It doubles as the phone key callers press to navigate — changing it affects the live menu. Reordering is a deliberate separate mode, not an accidental inline edit.

Pattern: Smart deactivation

Deactivating a category with active products triggers a modal with a forced choice: reassign products to another category, or deactivate them too. No silent data loss — the system requires a conscious decision before anything disappears.

04c — Solution · Mailing Module

Email campaigns, built from nothing

Before I joined, B2B clients had no way to send marketing or operational emails from within NBS. Communication happened outside the system entirely. The Mailing module was a greenfield build.

I designed the full end-to-end flow: template creation, mailing group management, sending flows (both "start from a template" and "start from a group"), and a per-email statistics dashboard.

Two sending entry points

Admins could start from an email template (then pick recipients) or start from a mailing group (then pick a template). Both paths felt natural, and neither blocked the other.

Statistics that teach

Per-email stats show open rate, click rate, delivery status, and engagement timeline. Admins can see if a promotional email drove results — and learn what works for this audience specifically.

What this unlocked

For the first time, B2B clients could measure whether their communications were landing. A store could see that 108 out of 224 recipients opened a Passover sale email — and 27 marked it as spam, a signal to revisit tone and content for this community.

05 — How I Measured

How I measured

Formal A/B testing and instrumented analytics weren't available in this environment. I worked with two proxies: a support complaint log I set up from scratch (the first structured feedback channel on the project), and structural workflow analysis — mapping the exact steps required for a task before and after each redesign. Where the PM reported qualitative improvement, I could often trace it to a specific, countable reduction in steps or decision points.

06 — Outcomes

Qualitative signals, honestly reported

Formal A/B testing wasn't possible in this environment. The outcomes below are drawn from PM feedback from client-facing conversations, stakeholder reactions, and observable changes in how the system was used.

Feedback came through the PM from both sides of the platform — B2B administrators configuring the system, and business owners (B2C-facing clients) using it to manage their day-to-day operations.

IVR navigation clarity

Stakeholders reported that IVR configuration became significantly easier to understand and explain to new client administrators after the redesign.

Audio cascade issue resolved

The new "Update Record Assignment" flow eliminated the class of errors where a recording change silently broke unrelated IVR steps.

First email analytics capability

Mailing module introduced measurable communication for the first time — clients could now see open rates, clicks, and unsubscribes per campaign.

Category management

Built from scratch as a dual-purpose structure: it organises product browsing on Shoppi and maps directly to the IVR phone tree. Display order = the key a caller presses. Admins can now manage all three levels of the hierarchy from one panel without losing context.

Product management feels structured

Small and mid-sized business owners reported via the PM that managing products had become noticeably more organised. Clear information hierarchy and consistent editing patterns made day-to-day updates faster and less error-prone.

Research infrastructure established

There was no structured way to collect user feedback before this project. By the end, support was documenting complaints by module, giving the team its first reliable signal for prioritisation.

06 — What I Learned

Reflections

1

Constraints breed creativity in research. Not having access to users forced me to develop richer ways of extracting insight — from PRDs, from PM conversations, from edge cases in business logic. It made me a more resourceful researcher.

2

"Add more fields" is almost never the answer. Across every module, the pattern held: the request was for more options, the real problem was lack of clarity about existing ones. Learning to surface this reframe early saved multiple false-start design cycles.

3

Reducing features doesn't reduce confusion for non-technical users. Many of the platform's administrators aren't comfortable with computers to begin with. Limiting what they can do doesn't improve their experience — it removes their sense of control, which makes things worse. I kept coming back to this when advocating for richer functionality over simplified but restrictive flows.

4

Legacy systems teach you about real user behaviour at scale. Working on a product with years of accumulated decisions — some good, some not — gave me an understanding of how design debt compounds, and why consistency in edit/view state is worth defending every single time.

5

If I could do it again: I'd push earlier for even lightweight usability proxies — even if that meant structured written questionnaires via the PM, or session recordings with client admins who were comfortable being observed. The cultural constraints were real, but not absolute.
